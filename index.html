2. Project Proposal

2.1 Project Name

AccessMap (Accessible Navigation Map)

2.2 Project Background & Objectives

Current mainstream navigation apps (e.g., Baidu Maps, Amap, Apple Maps) lack dynamic and user-specific accessibility information. People with mobility impairments—such as wheelchair users, visually impaired individuals, the elderly with arthritis, and temporarily injured people—often encounter barriers like blocked accessible ramps, out-of-service elevators, and discontinuous tactile paving during travel, leading to travel frustration or even giving up going out.

The core objective of this project is to design the AccessMap app, which integrates crowdsourced data, sensor feedback, and smart device integration to provide real-time, reliable, and personalized accessible route planning. It aims to solve the problem of insufficient dynamic accessibility information in current mapping services and improve the travel convenience and safety of people with mobility impairments.

2.3 Target Users

  • Long-term mobility-impaired groups: Wheelchair users, visually impaired individuals (using canes or screen-reading software)
  • Age-related mobility-impaired groups: Elderly people over 60 with mild arthritis (using canes)
  • Temporary mobility-impaired groups: People with fractures or ligament strains (using crutches temporarily)
  • Indirect users: Ordinary people who take care of relatives with mobility impairments

2.4 Core Functions (Preliminary Design)

  • Real-time accessibility status update: Display real-time status of facilities like elevators, ramps, and tactile paving (e.g., "elevator in normal use," "ramp blocked")
  • Personalized route planning: Customize routes based on user types (e.g., wheelchair users avoid steps, visually impaired users prioritize continuous tactile paving)
  • One-click emergency assistance: Connect to family members, 120 emergency services, or nearby volunteers with real-time location sharing
  • Voice + vibration navigation: Provide clear voice prompts (with adjustable speed) and smartwatch vibration reminders (left turn vibrates left, right turn vibrates right) for visually impaired users and wheelchair users
  • Crowdsourced information reporting: Allow users to upload photos or text reports of accessibility barriers (e.g., "narrow ramp at convenience store") with simplified operation (e.g., voice reporting for the elderly)

3. Data Collection Plan

To verify user needs and pain points, and provide a basis for app function design, we conducted two types of data collection: user interviews and questionnaires. The detailed plan and implementation results are as follows:

3.1 Interview Plan

3.1.1 Interview Objectives

Deeply understand the travel pain points, existing navigation app experience, and functional expectations of core target users (wheelchair users, visually impaired individuals, the elderly, temporarily injured people).

3.1.2 Interview Objects

  • 1 wheelchair user (64-year-old Ms. Li, long-term wheelchair user)
  • 1 visually impaired individual (45-year-old Mr. Zhang, congenital visual impairment, using cane + screen-reading software)
  • 1 elderly person (68-year-old Grandpa Wang, mild arthritis, using cane)
  • 1 temporarily injured person (28-year-old Mr. Zhao, left leg fracture, using crutches for 1 month)
  • Total: 4 potential users, covering all core target user groups

3.1.3 Interview Process & Content

  • Intro (5 mins): Explain the purpose of the interview, duration (20-25 mins), confidentiality commitment, and obtain user consent for recording
  • Warm-up (5 mins): Understand daily travel methods, travel scenarios (e.g., going to hospital, shopping), and use of current navigation apps
  • Main Body (15 mins): Focus on 3 topics with follow-up questions:
    • Topic 1: Mobility challenges (recent travel barriers, coping methods, cases of giving up going out)
    • Topic 2: Information trust (ways to confirm accessibility facilities, trust in user-shared information, preferences for voice/vibration prompts)
    • Topic 3: Functional expectations (priority factors for route planning, willingness to participate in crowdsourcing, interface interaction suggestions)
  • Closing (5 mins): Collect additional user suggestions and thank users for their participation

3.1.4 Interview Results

Completed 4 interview transcripts, collected key insights such as:

  • Wheelchair users need real-time updates on elevator/ramp status and accessible toilet locations
  • Visually impaired individuals require accurate voice prompts with distance information (e.g., "turn left after 10 meters") and vibration navigation
  • The elderly prefer large buttons, large fonts, and a "senior mode" (simplified functions like "check bus," "find toilet")
  • Temporarily injured people expect route planning to avoid steps/narrow ramps and link to ride-hailing software

3.2 Survey Plan

3.2.1 Survey Objectives

Quantify user needs, verify interview insights, and understand the willingness to use the app and functional preferences of a larger range of potential users.

3.2.2 Survey Objects

  • Total: 10 potential users, covering 5 user types:
    • 2 wheelchair users
    • 2 visually impaired individuals
    • 3 elderly people (over 60)
    • 2 temporarily injured people
    • 1 ordinary person (taking care of wheelchair-using relatives)

3.2.3 Survey Content

  • Basic information: Age group, user type, weekly travel frequency, commonly used navigation apps
  • Accessibility needs: Frequency of confirming accessibility routes before going out, pain points of current navigation apps
  • Functional preferences: Willingness to use the AccessMap app, importance ranking of core functions (e.g., real-time status, one-click emergency assistance)
  • Interaction preferences: Preferred text size, interaction methods (e.g., touch screen, voice control), trust in user-shared information
  • Concerns & suggestions: Worries about crowdsourced information (e.g., outdated information, privacy leakage) and suggestions for app optimization

3.2.4 Survey Results

Collected 10 sets of raw questionnaire data, with key quantitative conclusions:

  • 80% of respondents are "very willing" or "relatively willing" to use the AccessMap app
  • Top 3 important functions: Real-time elevator/ramp status (8 users ranked top 2), one-click emergency assistance (100% of the elderly/temporarily injured ranked top 3), voice + vibration navigation (100% of visually impaired individuals ranked top 1)
  • Interaction preferences: The elderly prefer "larger text" + "voice control"; visually impaired individuals prefer "adjustable text size" + "gesture + voice"; others prefer "standard text size" + "touch screen control"
  • Crowdsourcing willingness: 100% of wheelchair users/visually impaired individuals are willing to participate in crowdsourcing; 60% of the elderly are unwilling (due to complex operations)
